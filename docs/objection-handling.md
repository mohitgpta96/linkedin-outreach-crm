# Objection Handling — LinkedIn Outreach Responses

## How to Use This Doc
When a prospect responds with hesitation, pushback, or a "no",
Claude should reference this doc to draft an appropriate reply.

Golden Rule: NEVER argue. NEVER re-pitch aggressively.
Every objection response should be gracious, brief, and leave
the door open without being pushy.

---

## Objection 1: "We already have a PM / We're not looking for one"

**What they're really saying:**
They don't see a gap in their current setup, OR they have someone
and don't want to replace them.

**Response Strategy:**
Acknowledge → Validate → Plant a seed → Exit gracefully

**Example Response:**
"That's great — sounds like you've got that covered. If you ever
hit a scaling crunch where your PM needs backup on a specific
initiative, I do fractional work that layers in without disrupting
existing workflows. But no need right now — happy to just stay
connected and exchange ideas."

**What NOT to say:**
- "But a freelance PM could add so much value..." (arguing)
- "Are you sure? Most teams I talk to are overwhelmed..." (dismissive)

---

## Objection 2: "We don't have the budget for this"

**What they're really saying:**
Either genuinely tight on budget, OR they don't see enough value
to justify spending.

**Response Strategy:**
Empathize → Reframe value → Offer a low/no-cost entry point

**Example Response:**
"Totally understand — budget is real, especially at your stage. Tell
you what — I've got a project health checklist I use with teams in
similar situations. Takes 10 minutes to go through and usually
surfaces 2-3 quick wins. Happy to share it, no cost. Worst case,
you get a useful tool."

**What NOT to say:**
- "I can lower my rates..." (devalues you immediately)
- "Think of the ROI..." (sounds like a sales script)

---

## Objection 3: "Can you send me more information?"

**What they're really saying:**
Could be genuine interest, OR could be a polite way to end
the conversation. Treat it as genuine but keep it light.

**Response Strategy:**
Send something specific and bounded → Include a soft next step

**Example Response:**
"Sure! Here's a quick one-pager on how I typically work with
teams at your stage — covers scope, timeline, and what the
first 2 weeks look like. [link or attachment]

If anything resonates, happy to do a quick 15-min walkthrough
tailored to your setup. And if the timing isn't right, no
worries at all."

**What NOT to say:**
- Sending a 10-page proposal (too much, too early)
- "Let me know when you've reviewed it" (creates pressure)

---

## Objection 4: "We prefer to hire full-time"

**What they're really saying:**
They want continuity and commitment, which they associate
with full-time hires.

**Response Strategy:**
Validate their preference → Show how fractional addresses
their real concern → Offer a bridge scenario

**Example Response:**
"Makes total sense — full-time gives you dedicated focus. A lot
of teams I've worked with actually brought me in fractionally
while they were hiring full-time — I kept things moving and
even helped define the PM role spec so they hired the right
person. Just something to keep in mind if the hiring timeline
stretches out."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Freelance is actually better because..." (arguing their preference)
- "Full-time PMs are overrated..." (insulting)

---

## Objection 5: "I'm not the right person to talk to"

**What they're really saying:**
Either genuinely not the decision-maker, OR deflecting.

**Response Strategy:**
Thank them → Ask for a warm intro → Make it easy for them

**Example Response:**
"Appreciate you letting me know! Would you be open to pointing
me toward the right person on your team? Happy to reach out
separately — and of course, I'd mention you connected us.
No pressure at all if that doesn't feel right."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Can you forward my message to them?" (lazy, puts burden on them)
- "Who is the decision-maker?" (too direct, feels like a sales call)

---

## Objection 6: "We've had bad experiences with freelancers"

**What they're really saying:**
Trust is broken. They've been burned by unreliable freelancers
who disappeared, missed deadlines, or delivered poor quality.

**Response Strategy:**
Validate their experience → Differentiate through specifics →
Offer a risk-free way to test

**Example Response:**
"That's frustrating — and honestly, I hear it more than I'd
like. Bad freelancer experiences usually come down to lack of
structure and accountability. What I do differently: weekly
deliverable check-ins, transparent time tracking, and a clear
exit clause if it's not working after the first 2 weeks. But I
get the hesitation — happy to start with a small bounded task
so you can see the work quality firsthand."

**What NOT to say:**
- "I'm not like other freelancers" (everyone says this)
- "That won't happen with me" (unverifiable promise)

---

## Objection 7: "Let me think about it / Not right now"

**What they're really saying:**
Could be genuine timing issue, OR polite rejection.
Either way, respect it.

**Response Strategy:**
Acknowledge → Set a soft future touchpoint → Exit clean

**Example Response:**
"Absolutely — no rush at all. I'll circle back in a month or
so to see if anything's shifted. In the meantime, feel free
to reach out if something comes up. Happy to be a resource
even informally."

**Follow-Up Rule:**
- Set a reminder for 30 days
- When following up, lead with NEW value (not "just checking in")
- If they say "not now" twice, mark as passive — engage only
  through content (likes, comments) for 3+ months

**What NOT to say:**
- "When would be a good time?" (too pushy)
- "Just checking in" after 30 days (empty, annoying)

---

## Objection 8: "Your rates are too high"

**What they're really saying:**
They either have a fixed budget below your range, OR they
don't yet see enough value to justify the cost.

**Response Strategy:**
Don't drop rates immediately → Reframe around value and
outcomes → Offer a scoped-down option

**Example Response:**
"I appreciate the transparency. My rates reflect the speed and
structure I bring — most clients see ROI in the first 2 sprints
through reduced delivery friction. That said, if budget is tight,
we could start with a scoped project — say, a 2-week sprint
setup and process audit — so you can see the impact before
committing to ongoing work. Would something like that fit better?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "I can offer a discount" (instant credibility loss)
- "You get what you pay for" (condescending)
- "My other clients pay this rate" (irrelevant to them)

---

## Objection 9: "We use [tool X], not [tool Y]"

**What they're really saying:**
They're worried about compatibility or switching costs.

**Response Strategy:**
Show flexibility → Name-drop your tool range → Reframe
to process over tools

**Example Response:**
"No problem at all — I'm tool-agnostic. I've managed teams
on Jira, Asana, Trello, MS Project, Smartsheet, Notion, and
Linear. I adapt to whatever your team already uses. The
methodology and process matter more than the tool — and
I'll work within your existing stack."

---

## Objection 10: "What's your experience in [specific industry]?"

**What they're really saying:**
They want domain relevance. They're worried you won't
understand their context.

**Response Strategy:**
Be honest → Bridge to transferable skills → Offer to
demonstrate understanding quickly

**Example Response (if you have relevant experience):**
"Good question. I've worked on [relevant projects] in [adjacent
space]. The core PM challenges — sprint management, stakeholder
alignment, delivery tracking — are very similar. I also ramp up
fast on domain specifics. Happy to do a quick 15-min chat where
I can show you I understand the context."

**Example Response (if no direct experience):**
"I'll be honest — I haven't worked in [exact industry] before.
But I've managed projects in [adjacent spaces], and the execution
challenges are very transferable. What I find is that strong
process and communication solve 80% of delivery problems regardless
of domain. If you're open to it, I could review your current setup
and show you where I'd add value — takes 15 minutes."

**What NOT to say:**
- Pretending you have experience you don't (they'll find out)
- "Industry doesn't matter" (dismissive of their concern)

---

## Universal Response Principles

1. **Acknowledge first, always.** Show you heard them before responding.

2. **Never argue.** If they push back twice, respect it and move on.

3. **Keep responses under 100 words** when handling objections in DMs.

4. **Always leave the door open** but never leave it creaky
   (don't keep pushing it).

5. **Log every objection** in prospects/outreach-log.csv —
   patterns reveal ICP or messaging problems.

6. **If an objection comes up 5+ times**, update the messaging
   playbook and consider whether the outreach approach needs
   fundamental changes, not just better replies.
